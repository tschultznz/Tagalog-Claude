TAGALOG CURRICULUM

Purpose:
This curriculum turns Tom’s existing Glossika-based Tagalog familiarity into active spoken production and then extends it into broader everyday Tagalog.
It is ordered by practical usefulness, structural clarity, and reusability.
It is not meant to be a rigid school syllabus.
The tutor should move forward gradually while continuously reviewing old material.

GENERAL CURRICULUM RULES

- Move from high-frequency practical patterns to broader variation.
- Review older modules constantly.
- Teach one main target block at a time.
- A session may cover part of one module, one full module, or more than one light section depending on production stability.
- Prefer production and transformation over passive explanation.
- Stay aligned with the Glossika corpus whenever possible.
- Do not introduce difficult focus/theory too early.
- Do not move ahead just because a pattern was seen once.
- Use more of the corpus over time: more verbs, more objects, more people, more situations, more daily-life domains.
- Treat modules as mastery targets, not rigid session boxes.

==================================================
STAGE 1. FOUNDATION
==================================================

Purpose:
Stage 1 establishes the practical operating system for active production:
- existence and possession
- clitics and pronouns
- negation
- question forms
- particles
- modal basics
- location and movement
- time
- high-frequency AF
- light OF awareness
- short practical dialogue

==================================================
MODULE 1. MAY / WALA / BASIC EXISTENCE
==================================================

Main goals:
- talk about what exists
- talk about what is available
- talk about what is missing
- begin simple possession statements

Core targets:
- may + noun
- mayroon + noun
- wala + noun
- wala + possessor
- may + question forms

Examples:
- May tao.
- May pagkain.
- Wala akong pera.
- Wala siyang oras.
- May oras ka ba?

Student should be able to:
- say what there is
- say what there is not
- ask simple availability questions

==================================================
MODULE 2. PRONOUNS AND CLITIC PLACEMENT
==================================================

Main goals:
- get used to short pronoun forms
- reduce English-style word order
- build natural sentence rhythm

Core targets:
- ako / ka / siya / kami / tayo / kayo / sila
- ko / mo / niya / namin / natin / ninyo / nila
- clitic placement in short sentences
- common contractions like kong, mong, niyang

Examples:
- Gusto kong kumain.
- Ayaw niyang umalis.
- Kailangan naming pumunta.
- Alam mo ba?

Student should be able to:
- produce more natural short sentences
- stop placing pronouns in English-like positions

==================================================
MODULE 3. NEGATION
==================================================

Main goals:
- distinguish different kinds of negation
- avoid using one negation form for everything

Core targets:
- hindi
- wala
- ayaw
- practical contrast between them

Examples:
- Hindi ako pagod.
- Wala akong pera.
- Ayaw kong sumama.
- Hindi ko alam.

Student should be able to:
- say no
- deny facts
- express absence
- express refusal or unwillingness

MODULE 11. CONTROLLED VARIATION
==================================================

Main goals:
- turn one known sentence into many useful related sentences
- build flexibility without cognitive overload

Core targets:
For one anchor sentence, change only one thing at a time:
- pronoun
- negation
- particle
- time word
- location
- noun slot
- verb slot
- question form

Example anchor:
- Gusto kong kumain ngayon.

Variations:
- Gusto mong kumain ngayon.
- Ayaw kong kumain ngayon.
- Gusto kong kumain mamaya.
- Gusto ko nang kumain ngayon.
- Gusto mo bang kumain ngayon?

Student should be able to:
- generate multiple correct variations from one known frame

==================================================
STAGE 2. NEW BASIC CONTENT ON TOP OF THE FOUNDATION
==================================================

Purpose:
Stage 2 introduces new high-frequency everyday content beyond the Stage 1 base while continuing to recycle Stage 1.
It should create real Tagalog growth, not just harder drills on Stage 1.

Stage 2 design rules:
- each module introduces one main new high-frequency content block
- every module should recycle 2 to 3 older Stage 1 supports
- every module must activate 1 verb cluster, 1 lexical field, and 3 to 8 additional useful corpus items where appropriate
- do not stay trapped in the same small set of movement and modal verbs
- broaden active use of everyday domains such as home, meals, shopping, transport, work, study, weather, health, errands, services, money, objects, and social plans
- do not make naka-, nakapag-, ma-, pag-, and makipag- into primary Stage 2 module targets
- when such forms appear in useful anchor sentences, teach them as practical chunks or quick decodes only
- reserve systematic family expansion for the next curriculum

==================================================
STAGE 3. VOCABULARY EXPANSION AND CONTROLLED FAMILY GROWTH
==================================================

Purpose:
Stage 3 widens Tom’s active everyday vocabulary and starts the next practical layer of the verb system without losing Stage 1 and Stage 2 control.
It is not a jump into full grammar theory.
It is a corpus-based expansion stage built around domains, reusable chunks, and carefully limited family growth.

Stage 3 design rules:
- keep Stage 1 and Stage 2 alive in every session
- vocabulary breadth is now a primary target, not a side effect
- each module should combine:
  - 1 lexical domain
  - 1 practical verb cluster or family
  - 3 to 8 additional high-value items from the corpus
- use family expansion only when it clearly improves speech, not for theory completeness
- prioritize high-frequency, adult, daily-life speech
- move from chunks to light decoding only after stable repeated use
- keep lessons short and production-heavy
- use AF/OF review as support, not as the main topic unless needed
- do not turn Stage 3 into a giant affix survey
- reserve broad taxonomy and rare derivations for a later stage if needed

STAGE 4. COMMUNICATIVE FLUENCY, FINAL CLEANUP, AND PHRASE AUTOMATION
==================================================

Purpose:
Stage 4 turns Tom’s current structural control into faster, broader, more usable speech.
It is the bridge from:
- “I understand the pattern”
to
- “I can actually use Tagalog in daily life.”

This is not a return to long grammar study.
It is a phrase-led, domain-led, conversation-led stage with one final compact grammar cleanup.

STAGE 4 DESIGN RULES

- phrase first, explanation second
- keep explanations compressed and structural
- every new phrase must be introduced as a compressed pattern:
  - frame
  - practical meaning
  - one or two slot options
  - one contrast when useful
- review 8 to 12 old phrases every session
- add only 3 to 6 new useful items per session
- keep one live domain for 2 to 4 sessions before rotating
- recycle old domains on purpose
- grammar appears only when it unlocks a phrase Tom needs
- do not treat Stage 4 as a giant affix stage
- do not stop adding useful vocabulary just because a grammar point is not perfect
- use mini-dialogues, service exchanges, and short real-life scenes constantly
- keep the language adult, practical, spoken, and useful for travel, daily life, and relationship talk
- prepare for real use in the Philippines, including short exchanges, repair language, and travel-logistics speech

SESSION LOAD RULE

A normal Stage 4 session should contain:
- 1 carryover weak point
- 8 to 12 old phrases
- 3 to 5 anchors from one domain
- 3 to 6 new lexical items or chunks
- 4 to 6 controlled substitutions
- 3 to 5 short dialogue turns
- 1 short repair drill
- 1 short narration or recap line

PROGRESSION RULE FOR STAGE 4

Move forward when:
- Tom can use the main chunk in a short practical exchange
- Tom can keep the core meaning stable under small lexical changes
- Tom can retrieve the phrase without long hesitation
- Tom can use the phrase in at least two domains or scenes
- Tom still keeps pronouns, particles, and negation reasonably stable

Do not move forward if:
- the phrase only works in one memorized sentence
- the tutor is explaining more than Tom is producing
- the student cannot yet answer short follow-up questions with the target chunk
- new vocabulary is being added faster than it is being recycled
